## Scoring Breakdown

### Problem Severity: 6/10 (×2 = 12 points)
**Justification:** Managing creator sponsorships is a messy combination of content calendars, contract terms, and deliverables. For multi-person teams (e.g., YouTube creators with editors), the lack of performance attribution is a real pain point. However, spreadsheets are the standard workaround. Not a "hair-on-fire" problem, but a growing organizational friction for successful creators.

### Market Signal Strength: 5/10 (×2 = 10 points)
**Justification:** 3 signals from 3 distinct sources (Reddit r/PartneredYoutube, Stripe, InfluenceFlow). One DIY indicator (YouTuber built custom system). However, engagement metrics are unknown, and 2 of the 3 signals are from industry articles, not direct user complaints.

### Solution Gap: 6/10 (×2 = 12 points)
**Justification:** YouTube Studio provides basic stats but lacks team attribution (editor performance tracking). Stripe handles payments but has no content or contract context. InfluenceFlow exists but the need for "complete guides" suggests the tool doesn't solve it all. Gap is real for "professional" creators, but may be too niche.

### Monetization Clarity: 6/10 (×1.5 = 9 points)
**Justification:** Professional creators (YouTube partners, large podcasters) have established budgets for tools and clearly struggle with contract/payment tracking (Stripe signal). Willingness-to-pay is likely $30-100/mo. However, the total addressable market (TAM) of "professional creators with teams" is relatively small compared to broader SMB tools.

### Build Feasibility: 7/10 (×1.5 = 10.5 points)
**Justification:** Building a dashboard that pulls YouTube/Podcast API data and connects it to a contract database is straightforward. No complex math or AI required. The challenge is the "integration surface area" (YouTube, Spotify, Apple Podcasts, Stripe, all need to be connected).

### Channel Accessibility: 4/10 (×1 = 4 points)
**Justification:** Creators are notoriously hard to reach and sell to. They are bombarded with pitches from editors, agencies, and tools. Distribution is the biggest hurdle here. No "central community" for professional creators that isn't already oversaturated with pitches.

## Skeptic's Take
"Selling to creators" is where startups go to die. While the organizational mess of sponsorship tracking is real, most creators solve it with a spreadsheet and a part-time VA. The TAM for "sophisticated creator teams needing editor-level performance attribution" is incredibly small. Unless this can be expanded into a broader creator-business-OS, it's a feature, not a company.
